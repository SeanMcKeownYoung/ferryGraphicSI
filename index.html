Who's to blame for Staten Island Ferry service issues?

The Staten Island Ferry, once regarded as one of the most reliable forms of mass transit in New York City, has fallen into disarray in recent years. The Department of Transportation's inability to recruit and retain qualified crew members during a national maritime workers shortage has resulted in regular service disruptions, particularly during afternoon and overnight hours. The Marine Engineers Beneficial Association, which represents a sizable portion of the Ferry's workforce, has argued that New York City's continued refusal to provide crew members with a “fair and respectful” new contract has contributed to the staffing issues, and subsequently, the service disruptions.

New York City Department of Transportation
The New York City Department of Transportation is responsible for operating the Staten Island Ferry, one of the largest passenger ferry systems in the country. In recent years, the Department of Transportation has struggled to recruit and retain crew members, leading to insufficient staffing levels that have forced recurring service reductions due to the department's inability to adequately staff all scheduled trips.

Marine Engineers Beneficial Association (MEBA)
The Marine Engineers Beneficial Association is an international maritime union that represents the captains, assistant captains, mates, engineers and chief engineers on the Staten Island Ferry. The union has been fighting the city for nearly 13 years for a new contract, with workers not having received a wage increase since 2010 and union officials c laiming the lack of a new contract and existing low wages have made it more difficult for the city to recruit and retain workers during the ongoing national maritime workers shortage.
Mayor Eric Adams
New York City Mayor Eric Adams is responsible for administering all city agencies, including the Department of Transportation, which operates the Staten Island Ferry, the Office of Labor Relations, which is responsible for negotiating union contracts, and the Office of Management and Budget, which determines city funding for various agencies. MEBA had expressed cautious optimism that Mayor Adams would prioritize resolving the longstanding contract dispute, but little progress has been made since he took office in January 2022.
New York City Office of Labor Relation
The New York City Office of Labor Relations represents Mayor Eric Adams in all labor relations between the City of New York and the labor unions that represent city employees, like the ones who operate the Staten Island Ferry. Over the past decade, the Office of Labor Relations has reached contract agreements with dozens of unions, with the notable exception of the Marine Engineers Beneficial Association.
New York City Office of Management & Budget
The New York City Office of Management and Budget develops and executes NYC's budget, which is the largest municipal budget in the United States. The office determines the amount of funding that will be issued to each city agency annually and how that funding will be used. Additional funding would likely need to be provided to the Department of Transportation to accommodate the higher wages being sought by Staten Island Ferry workers.
New York City Council
The New York City Council introduces and votes on city legislation, negotiates the City's budget with the mayor and monitors city agencies, like the Department of Transportation, to make sure they're effectively serving New Yorkers. Despite the repeated service disruptions on the Staten Island Ferry, which are in violation of Local Law 88 of 2013 requiring half-hourly service, the City Council has done little to hold the Department of Transportation accountable.
United States Coast Guard
The United States Coast Guard sets forth the licensing requirements and necessary certifications for Staten Island Ferry crew members. The Coast Guard also mandates that each vessel be staffed with at least one captain, one assistant captain and various other positions, like chief marine engineers, marine engineers and marine oilers. These safety mandates, coupled with the ongoing staffing issues, have been the driving force behind the recurring Staten Island Ferry service disruptions.
